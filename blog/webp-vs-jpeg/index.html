WebP vs JPEG: Which Format Makes Images Smaller in 2026?

Short answer: WebP produces files roughly 25–34% smaller than JPEG at the same visual quality, works in every modern browser, and supports transparency. JPEG still wins for legacy workflows and universal upload compatibility.

What is the difference between WebP and JPEG?

JPEG has been the default photographic format on the web since the 1990s. It compresses images by discarding detail the human eye is unlikely to notice, which works well for photos but shows artifacts at aggressive settings. WebP, developed by Google, uses more modern prediction-based compression borrowed from video codecs. The practical result: at the same file size, WebP usually looks better; at the same quality, WebP is usually meaningfully smaller.

WebP also does two things JPEG simply cannot: it stores alpha transparency (like PNG) and animation (like GIF), making it a single format that can replace three older ones for most web use.

File size comparison

Google's own studies found lossy WebP files average 25–34% smaller than JPEG at equivalent perceptual quality. Real-world results vary by image: photos with smooth gradients and soft backgrounds compress dramatically better in WebP, while extremely detailed, noisy images show smaller gains. The only reliable way to know is to convert your own image and compare — our JPG to WebP converter shows the before/after size for every file, entirely in your browser.

Quality: can you see the difference?

At a quality setting of 80–90, most people cannot distinguish a WebP from the JPEG original in a blind comparison, even though the WebP is significantly smaller. Below quality 60, both formats show artifacts, but they degrade differently: JPEG produces blocky 8×8 patterns, while WebP tends to smooth fine detail. For product photos, portfolio images, and blog illustrations, 85 is a safe default.

Browser and platform support

WebP support has been universal in browsers for years: Chrome, Firefox, Edge, Safari (since 2020), Opera, and all major mobile browsers render it natively. The remaining gaps are not browsers but platforms — some older CMS plugins, email clients, and upload forms still reject .webp files. Email is the most notable holdout: if the image goes into a newsletter, use JPEG.

SEO and Core Web Vitals impact

Google's PageSpeed Insights explicitly recommends serving images in "next-gen formats", and WebP is the primary one it means. Smaller images improve Largest Contentful Paint (LCP), one of the Core Web Vitals used in ranking. Cutting your image payload by a third is often the single cheapest performance win available on an image-heavy page.

When to choose which

Choose WebP for every image displayed on a website: hero images, product shots, thumbnails, blog images, and transparent UI graphics. Choose JPEG when the destination demands it — email newsletters, older upload systems, print pipelines — or when you're archiving originals that started life as JPEG. Frequently asked questions

Is WebP better than JPEG?

For most web images, yes. WebP is typically 25–34% smaller at comparable quality and additionally supports transparency and animation.

Does converting JPEG to WebP lose quality?

Lossy WebP re-encodes the image, so some data is discarded — the same as re-saving a JPEG. At quality 80–90 the difference is rarely visible. Always keep your original as the master.

Do all browsers support WebP?

Yes — Chrome, Firefox, Safari, Edge, and all major mobile browsers have supported WebP for years.

When should I keep using JPEG?

For email newsletters, uploads to systems that reject WebP, and archival masters that are already JPEG.
