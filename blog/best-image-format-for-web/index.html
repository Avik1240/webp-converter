Image formats

Best Image Format for Websites in 2026

The short version: WebP for photos and graphics, SVG for icons and logos, AVIF if your pipeline generates it automatically, JPEG and PNG only where compatibility demands them, GIF almost never.

The decision in one paragraph

If you remember one rule: vector artwork gets SVG, everything else gets WebP. That combination covers logos, icons, photos, screenshots, product images, and transparent graphics with the smallest files that every modern browser renders natively. The rest of this guide explains when the exceptions apply.

WebP — the default for 2026

WebP does the job of three legacy formats: smaller photos than JPEG, transparency like PNG, and animation like GIF — with universal browser support. For a typical content site, converting existing images to WebP is the single biggest payload reduction available. You can convert any image to WebP free in your browser, with no uploads and no limits.

AVIF — smaller still, with caveats

AVIF, based on the AV1 video codec, usually beats WebP by a further 10–30% on photographs and shines at low bitrates. Browser support is now solid across Chrome, Firefox, Safari, and Edge. The caveats are practical: encoding is slow, some tools and CMSs still can't produce it, and fine-detail images can look over-smoothed. Best strategy: let a CDN or build tool generate AVIF automatically with WebP as fallback via <picture>. Hand-managing AVIF files is rarely worth the effort.

JPEG — the compatibility workhorse

JPEG remains the format every system on earth accepts: email clients, ancient CMS uploads, print shops, government portals. Use it when the destination demands it, and keep JPEG originals as masters. For anything displayed on your own website, converting to WebP first is nearly always the right move — see the full WebP vs JPEG comparison.

PNG — lossless when it matters

PNG stores every pixel exactly. That matters for screenshots with small text, design assets that will be edited again, and pipelines that require lossless input. It does not matter for display on a web page, where a quality-90 WebP is visually identical and far smaller. Rule: PNG as a working format, WebP as a delivery format. Convert with the PNG to WebP tool.

SVG — everything vector

Logos, icons, charts, and simple illustrations should be SVG. Vectors scale perfectly on any screen density, are typically tiny, can be styled with CSS, and never pixelate. The only time to rasterize an SVG (to WebP or PNG) is when a platform refuses vector uploads.

GIF — retire it

A GIF animation is routinely five to ten times larger than the same clip as animated WebP or a muted looping video. Colors are limited to 256. In 2026 GIF survives on platform requirements and nostalgia, not merit. Convert with the GIF to WebP tool when you can.

Cheat sheet

Photos and screenshots for your site: WebP (AVIF if automated). Logos and icons: SVG. Email images: JPEG/PNG. Editable masters: PNG or original RAW/JPEG. Animation: animated WebP or video. When in doubt: WebP.

Frequently asked questions

What is the best image format for websites?

WebP as the default for photos and graphics, SVG for vector artwork, AVIF where your pipeline automates it.

Is AVIF better than WebP?

Usually smaller for photos, but slower to encode and less universally supported in tooling. WebP is the safer default; AVIF is an automated upgrade.

Should I still use PNG?

As a lossless working format, yes. For delivery on a website, WebP shows the same image at a fraction of the size.

Is GIF still worth using?

Rarely — animated WebP or muted video is far smaller at better quality.
